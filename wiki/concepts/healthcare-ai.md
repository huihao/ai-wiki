# Healthcare AI

## Definition

Healthcare AI refers to the application of artificial intelligence technologies—including machine learning, natural language processing, computer vision, and predictive modeling—to clinical decision-making, medical imaging, drug discovery, patient monitoring, and health informatics. It aims to improve diagnostic accuracy, treatment personalization, operational efficiency, and patient outcomes while navigating substantial challenges around data privacy, algorithmic fairness, model validation, and physician adoption.

## Key Aspects

- **Clinical decision support**: AI systems assist clinicians with diagnostic suggestions, treatment recommendations, risk stratification, and drug interaction alerts. These tools augment rather than replace physician judgment, with the goal of reducing medical errors and expanding access to specialist-level expertise in underserved settings.
- **Medical imaging AI**: One of the most mature application areas, covering automated radiology screening, pathology tissue classification, ophthalmology disease detection, and dermatology analysis. These systems often match or exceed human specialist accuracy on narrow, well-defined tasks but require rigorous validation before clinical deployment.
- **NLP for medical text**: Natural language processing automates electronic health record analysis, clinical documentation, medical literature mining, and patient communication processing—areas where clinician time is a critical bottleneck and where LLM-based extraction has dramatically improved capability.
- **Curated research infrastructure**: Maintaining up-to-date awareness of AI medicine research requires structured resources. The PCOM LibGuide (maintained by librarian Heidi Nance) curates RSS feeds from 10+ leading journals—JAMA AI, Nature Machine Intelligence, Radiology AI, Intelligence-Based Medicine—along with PubMed search feeds and Google AI Health updates, providing researchers and clinicians with a centralized entry point to the field's rapidly evolving literature.
- **Key challenges and risks**: Data privacy (HIPAA compliance), model validation and clinical safety, bias across demographic groups, interpretability of recommendations, and the gap between research performance and real-world clinical deployment all remain active concerns. AI models trained on non-representative datasets can perpetuate or amplify health disparities.
- **Regulatory and ethical landscape**: Healthcare AI operates under strict regulatory frameworks (FDA approval for clinical tools, IRB oversight for research) and requires careful attention to patient data protection, algorithmic transparency, human oversight for final decisions, and equitable access to AI-enhanced care across socioeconomic and demographic groups.
