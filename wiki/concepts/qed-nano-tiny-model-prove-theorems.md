# QED-Nano (Tiny Model Prove Theorems)

## Definition
QED-Nano refers to research on small language models (nano-scale) trained or fine-tuned to prove mathematical theorems, demonstrating that theorem proving capability can be achieved without the massive parameter counts of frontier LLMs. It represents a trend toward efficient, specialized models that apply formal reasoning in mathematics and logic.

## Key Aspects
- Demonstrates that theorem proving can be achieved with small, specialized models rather than large general-purpose LLMs
- Leverages formal proof systems (like Lean, Coq, or Isabelle) where proofs are machine-verifiable
- Tiny models can learn the syntactic patterns of formal proofs through training on existing proof corpora
- Represents a shift from "scale is all you need" toward efficiency and specialization in AI for mathematics
- Formal verification provides an unambiguous correctness signal: a proof either checks or it does not
- Applications include automated theorem proving, program verification, and mathematical discovery
- Highlights the potential for small, domain-specific models to outperform large models on narrow formal tasks
