# Friction in Learning

## Definition

Friction in learning refers to the productive difficulty and cognitive effort encountered when engaging deeply with new material, as opposed to the superficial ease of passive consumption. Research on learning methodology argues that friction — the struggle to parse arguments, verify claims, and build mental models — is precisely where durable understanding is formed, and that optimizing for comfort (e.g., using AI to summarize papers) often eliminates the very process that produces learning.

## Key Aspects

- **Intuition-Empirics-Details framework**: Mason Wang's paper-reading methodology (grasp intuition in 3 sentences, check empirical evidence via charts, dive into details only if warranted) uses friction strategically — applying effort where it produces the most learning while skipping friction that yields diminishing returns.
- **Sturgeon's Law application**: With 90% of papers being low-quality, speed becomes the default mode; friction is reserved for the 10% that demonstrate clear value, making selective depth a skill rather than a default behavior.
- **AI as a double-edged tool**: Using AI to understand specific concepts (e.g., clarifying a mathematical derivation) is valuable; using AI to summarize a paper eliminates the friction that produces genuine comprehension. The distinction is between using AI to amplify effort versus replacing it.
- **Chart quality as signal**: Visual charts serve as a proxy for research quality — they force authors to synthesize their argument into a clear visual form, and parsing them exercises the reader's analytical skills more productively than reading prose.
- **Prerequisite friction**: Encountering unfamiliar notation or concepts (e.g., attempting FlashAttention without understanding GPU architecture) produces frustration that signals a knowledge gap — climbing the "tree of ideas" upward to fill prerequisites is more productive than pushing forward blindly.
- **Deliberate difficulty**: The first few papers read should be "deliberately hard and long" (read line-by-line), building a foundation of deep comprehension before adopting faster sampling strategies — friction in early learning stages builds the taste needed to skip it later.
