# Principle of Computational Equivalence

## Definition

The Principle of Computational Equivalence (PCE) is a hypothesis proposed by Stephen Wolfram stating that almost all processes that are not obviously simple are computationally equivalent in their computational sophistication. This means that processes arising in nature, in social systems, or in engineered systems generally achieve roughly the same level of computational complexity — they are all capable of universal computation. The principle has deep implications: it suggests that there is no meaningful hierarchy of computational power among non-trivial systems, and that simple programs can produce behavior as complex as any computational process.

## Key Proponents / Critics

- [[stephen-wolfram]] — Originator of the Principle, presented in "A New Kind of Science" (2002)
- Critics argue the principle is difficult to formalize or falsify, and that it conflates different notions of computational complexity

## Evolution

- **1985**: Wolfram begins exploring computational equivalence through cellular automata experiments
- **2002**: The principle is formally articulated in "A New Kind of Science"
- **2010s**: The principle gains renewed attention in discussions about AI, consciousness, and the limits of computation
- **2023-2024**: Wolfram discusses the PCE in the context of LLMs and large-scale neural computation
