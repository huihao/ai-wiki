# Interpolation

## Definition

Interpolation, in the context of goal-setting and personal development, is the technique of working backward from a distant, ambitious goal to progressively nearer milestones by estimating what intermediate states must be true. Popularized by the "Wafflehouse Method," interpolation transforms a 5-year vision into concrete 3-year, 1-year, and daily targets by asking what must be approximately true at each intermediate point.

## Key Aspects

- **Backward Planning**: Start from the most ambitious goal (5 years out) and work backward, asking "what must be true 2 years before this happens?"
- **Progressive Refinement**: Each interpolation step (5-year to 3-year to 1-year to monthly to weekly to daily) makes goals progressively more concrete and actionable
- **DAG-Based Planning**: Goals are structured as a directed acyclic graph where steps depend on other steps; some "knot points" are actually multiple steps mashed together
- **Assume Every Day is a Bad Day**: Plan for worst-case availability to ensure plans survive real-world disruption
- **Monthly-Weekly-Daily Cascade**: Once 1-year goals exist, interpolate monthly, then weekly, then daily; every Sunday, review and adjust the plan
- **Unknot Knot Points**: When a step seems unclear, research it -- it may be multiple sub-steps or based on false assumptions
- **Mathematical Analogy**: Named after linear interpolation between known data points; here the "known points" are the vision and current state
- **Liberation and Accountability**: Having a personal plan removes excuses -- there is no one to blame but yourself
