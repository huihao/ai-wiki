# Sturgeon's Law

## Definition

Sturgeon's Law, formulated by science fiction author Theodore Sturgeon, states that "90% of everything is crap." In the context of research taste and technical judgment, the law serves as a heuristic for calibrating expectations: the vast majority of work in any field — research papers, code, products, ideas — will be of low quality, and the skill lies in efficiently identifying the remaining 10% that is genuinely valuable.

## Key Aspects

- **Base rate calibration**: The law provides a realistic baseline expectation for quality distribution in any domain, preventing both naive optimism (everything published must be good) and cynical paralysis (nothing is worth reading).
- **Research taste**: The ability to rapidly distinguish the valuable 10% from the noise is what distinguishes experienced researchers and practitioners — this skill is developed through broad exposure and critical evaluation.
- **Efficient filtering**: Experienced practitioners develop heuristics for quickly assessing quality: venue reputation, author track record, methodology rigor, novelty of claims, and reproducibility of results.
- **Survivorship bias awareness**: Sturgeon's Law reminds us that what we see (published papers, released products) represents survivors of selection processes that may themselves be flawed, meaning the true quality distribution among all attempts is even more skewed.
- **Motivation for curation**: The law justifies the value of curators, reviewers, and synthesis authors who perform the filtering service of identifying and surfacing the high-quality minority.
- **Domain independence**: The principle applies universally across fields — from academic research to open-source software to corporate initiatives — making it a fundamental heuristic for technical decision-making.
