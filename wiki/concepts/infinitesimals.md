# Infinitesimals

Infinitesimals are quantities that are closer to zero than any standard real number but are not equal to zero. They form the conceptual foundation of differential calculus, where derivatives represent ratios of infinitesimal changes. The concept of "orders of smallness" (first order, second order, etc.) classifies how quickly a quantity approaches zero relative to another, which is essential for understanding limits, differentials, and asymptotic analysis.

## Key Ideas
- A first-order small quantity like 1/60 can be considered "small" in context
- Second-order smallness: a small fraction of a small fraction (e.g., 1/3600)
- Higher-order smallness compounds multiplicatively
- In calculus, dx and dy are infinitesimal increments used to define derivatives
- When quantities of sufficiently high order of smallness appear in sums, they can be safely ignored
