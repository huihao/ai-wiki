# AGI

## Definition

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to hypothetical AI systems possessing the ability to understand, learn, and apply intelligence across a wide range of tasks at a level comparable to or exceeding human capabilities. Unlike narrow AI, which is designed for specific tasks, AGI would be capable of transfer learning, reasoning under novelty, and performing any intellectual task a human can. The concept remains debated, with no agreed-upon formal definition or established timeline for its realization.

## Evolution

- **1956**: The term "artificial intelligence" coined at the Dartmouth Conference, establishing the field's aspirational goal
- **1997**: Shane Legg and Mark Gubrud coin the term "AGI" to distinguish general machine intelligence from narrow AI
- **2014**: [[deepmind]]'s AlphaGo demonstrates superhuman performance in Go, sparking renewed AGI optimism
- **2017**: The [[transformers]] is introduced in "Attention Is All You Need," laying groundwork for scalable general-purpose models
- **2020**: GPT-3 demonstrates [[few-shot-learning]] capabilities, leading to widespread discussion about emergent intelligence
- **2022**: [[chatgpt]] achieves mainstream adoption, reigniting AGI discourse in the public sphere
- **2023**: Geoffrey Hinton and other prominent researchers issue public warnings about existential AGI risks
- **2024-2025**: Reasoning models (o1, Claude) demonstrate multi-step problem solving, narrowing the perceived gap to AGI
