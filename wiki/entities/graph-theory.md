# Graph Theory

## Summary
Graph theory is a branch of mathematics and computer science that studies graphs -- mathematical structures used to model pairwise relations between objects. A graph consists of vertices (nodes) connected by edges (links), and can be directed or undirected, weighted or unweighted. Graph theory provides foundational concepts for algorithms, network analysis, and is increasingly important in machine learning through graph neural networks.

## Key Attributes
- **Type**: concept/field
- **Notable for**: providing the mathematical foundation for modeling relationships and networks across computer science and beyond
- **Other facts**: Leonhard Euler founded graph theory in 1736 with the Seven Bridges of Königsberg problem; fundamental concepts include paths, cycles, connectivity, and planarity; key algorithms include Dijkstra's shortest path, Kruskal's and Prim's minimum spanning tree, and network flow algorithms; modern applications include social network analysis, knowledge graphs, and graph neural networks

- Classical graph algorithms are well-understood, but scaling graph computations to massive graphs (billions of nodes) in distributed systems remains an active challenge. The gap between theoretical graph algorithms and practical graph processing systems (like GraphX, Neo4j) is still significant.
