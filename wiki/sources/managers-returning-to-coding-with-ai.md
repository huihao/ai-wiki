# Managers Returning to 'Coding' with AI

## Summary

An engineering manager reflects on the trend of managers returning to hands-on coding with AI tools, categorizing seven distinct groups of managers based on their motivations: those expected to code, those coding for entertainment, those staying grounded, those learning to lead better, those driven by FOMO, those avoiding change, and those with mixed motivations. The author places himself in group 4 (learning to lead better) and argues that clarity about which group you belong to is essential for organizational alignment.

## Key Takeaways

- **Seven manager groups** returning to coding with AI: (1) expected to code, (2) entertainment/hobby, (3) staying grounded, (4) nervous/curious/excited about leading better, (5) FOMO, (6) pre-2025 status quo, (7) mixed bag.
- **"Coding" is in quotes**: Modern manager "coding" means directing AI tools (Claude Code, Cursor), not writing code by hand. The person may tweak a few lines.
- **Group 1 risks**: Power dynamics imbalance when the team is more technically competent than the manager-authority making technical decisions.
- **Group 4 (the author's group)**: The core challenge — AI changes both *what* engineers build and *how* they work, making traditional feedback, coaching, and performance evaluation frameworks obsolete.
- **Group 5 (FOMO)**: Managers sending Slack messages copy-pasted from ChatGPT unchanged, sending huge AI-generated PRs for review — creating "the illusion of value" rather than actual value.
- **Key organizational insight**: A manager must be clear to their organization about which group they stand in, especially at work. Mixed signals confuse teams.
- **The fundamental shift**: Unlike cloud or mobile (which affected specific roles), AI changes *all* software engineers, making it impossible for managers to rely on their existing evaluation frameworks.
