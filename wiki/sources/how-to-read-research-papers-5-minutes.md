## Summary

Mason Wang's guide to reading research papers efficiently after three years of treating paper-reading as a chore. The framework: Intuition → Empirics → Details (in that order). Advocates for speed as default, ruthlessly optimizing your process, and using AI tools while maintaining hard thinking.

## Key Takeaways

- Framework: (1) grasp intuition in 3 sentences, (2) check empirics via charts, (3) dive into details only if worth it
- Sturgeon's Law applies: 90% of ML papers are crap — speed lets you see enough to build quality taste
- Speed is safer default: can always circle back, but can't recover time wasted on mediocre papers
- Charts are strong proxy for quality: clear visual storytelling distinguishes foundational from incremental work
- Use AI for understanding, but never allow AI for summaries — friction is where learning happens
- The tree is not that deep: every concept sits on branch of ideas, climb upward to fill gaps
- Three dimensions to optimize: WHAT (research taste), HOW (your process), WHERE (finding good papers)

## Raw Notes

- First few papers should be deliberately hard and long (line-by-line)
- After initial slog, apply sampling/skimming/streamlining
- Three-sentence TLDR + Two Cents subjective comment + ratings (appreciation/importance)
- Notion database for tracking papers (my bookshelf)
- Save prompts to paste whole paper → intuition/empirics/details bird's-eye view
- Paper mistakes exist even in influential works (Adam optimizer convergence error, FlashAttention-2 typo)
- Neurode example: marketing claims about ADHD, but sources show weak evidence and harm on sleep
- Reframing papers connect threads from different corners: Universal Hopfield Networks, Fast Weight Programmers, VQ is sparse MLP
- Prerequisites matter: don't try FlashAttention without understanding GPU architecture
